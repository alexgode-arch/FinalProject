Navigating the AI Regulation Patchwork

A Path Forward for American Innovation

Executive Summary

The United States is at a critical juncture regarding the governance of artificial intelligence. In 2024, state lawmakers introduced nearly 700 AI-related bills across 45 states, with 113 enacted into law—a dramatic increase from just 191 bills in 2023. This explosive growth in state-level AI regulation has created a fragmented compliance landscape threatening American innovation and disproportionately harming small businesses.

65% of small businesses are concerned about rising litigation and compliance costs from conflicting AI laws. California's regulations alone will impose nearly $16,000 in annual compliance costs on small businesses.

This policy brief recommends establishing a federal AI regulatory framework that preempts conflicting state regulations while preserving state authority to enforce consumer protection and anti-discrimination laws.

Background and Problem Statement

The lack of comprehensive federal legislation on AI created a vacuum that states have rushed to fill. In 2024, 46 states passed 238 new pieces of technology regulation, an astounding 163% increase from the previous year. This exploding regulation covers a wide range of topics: employment and credit decision-making using automated decisions, preventing algorithmic bias, deepfakes, and data privacy requirements.

Colorado was the first to enact broad legislation on AI, through SB-205, effective June 2026. California, Connecticut, and Virginia have also enacted various unique implementations, creating a patchwork of conflicting requirements.

Key Stakeholders

These include state and federal government agencies, technology companies ranging from major platforms to AI startups, small businesses using AI to scale operations, consumers, and civil society organizations looking for protection against AI-related harms, and investors who also assess regulatory uncertainty.

The Urgency

Research shows that Colorado's AI law alone could cost the state 40,000 jobs and $7 billion in economic output by 2030. When faced with regulations like those in Colorado and California, one-third of small business owners said they would scale down AI use, and another fifth would avoid AI altogether.

A startup facing a 200% increase in compliance costs could see its operating margin shift from profitable at 13% to a loss of -7%. Without standardized frameworks, companies cannot budget for compliance with accuracy, which creates legal uncertainty advantageous to large corporations and detrimental to innovation by startups.

Policy Options and Recommendation

Option 1: Status Quo (State-Led Regulation)

Allow states to continue the development of their own AI frameworks with no intervention by the federal government. While this allows for local tailoring and policy experimentation, it creates unsustainable compliance chaos, disproportionately harms small businesses, reduces American competitiveness, and provides no consistent consumer protections across states.

Option 2: Full Federal Preemption

Enact a single federal AI law that preempts all state regulations and establishes uniform national standards. This will eliminate compliance complexity, and consistency will exist, but it may be politically infeasible, overly rigid for evolving technology, and override beneficial state innovations.

Option 3: Federal Framework with State Enforcement Authority

Provide a federal framework of regulation for high-risk AI applications while states retain the right to enforce consumer protection and anti-discrimination laws. This would provide national standards of AI transparency and accountability, a consistent definition of "high-risk AI systems," preemption of conflicting state AI-specific regulations, and allow the states to enforce through traditional legal mechanisms.

Why This Option Is Best

This approach reduces compliance burden while being respectful of state interests in consumer protection. It also adheres to successful federal frameworks laid out in other areas, such as securities regulation. The economic evidence is clear: reducing conflicting AI laws will allow American businesses, especially small businesses, to innovate without fear of inadvertently running afoul of the law. Consumers retain strong protection through both federal standards and state enforcement.

More importantly, this bipartisan approach is politically achievable—89% of tech laws were enacted in states with trifecta governments; this suggests broad support for carefully crafted federal action.

Implementation would include risk-based standards for consequential decisions, mandatory transparency requirements, impact assessments, clear consumer opt-out mechanisms, safe harbor provisions, and technical assistance for small businesses.

Conclusion

The explosion in state AI regulation reflects very real concerns about algorithmic discrimination and privacy. However, this current fragmented approach imperils American leadership in AI while failing to deliver consistent consumer protections.

A federal framework that sets clear standards while preserving state enforcement authority offers the best path forward. The cost of inaction is measured in billions of dollars of economic impact, lost opportunities, and diminished American competitiveness.

Congress should act decisively in 2025 to establish this framework before the patchwork becomes irreversibly complex.
